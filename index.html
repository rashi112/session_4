Introduction

Weft knitting is a common method of creating fabrics where loops of yarn are interlaced horizontally, row by row. Each loop is drawn through a previous one to form interconnected rows, allowing the fabric to stretch in both directions. This process results in fabrics that are soft, flexible, and comfortable, making them ideal for garments like T-shirts, sweaters, and socks.

3.1 Some Definitions


Technically upright direction of a knitted fabrics


In knitting, the technically upright direction refers to the "wale" direction, which runs vertically along the length of the fabric. Here’s a breakdown of what this means:
1. Wales: These are the columns of stitches that run vertically in a knitted fabric. They are formed by stitches stacked one above the other in each column.

2. Courses: These are the rows of stitches that run horizontally across the width of the fabric.
So, the technically upright direction of a knitted fabric follows the direction of the wales. If you’re looking at a typical knit fabric, the wales align with the longitudinal grain, which is usually parallel to the edges of the fabric (often along the selvage).

The face loop stitch and its notation

In knitting, a face loop stitch refers to the stitch that appears as a knit stitch on the right (front) side of the fabric. It is one of the fundamental stitches in knitting, forming the smooth, "V"-shaped pattern on the surface.

3.3 Purl Fabrics


What Are Purl Fabrics? Purl fabrics are fabrics in which the surface primarily features purl stitches. The purl stitch is the counterpart to the knit stitch, forming horizontal bars across the fabric surface. This stitch is typically used to create a textured look and is the reverse side of the knit stitch. Key Characteristics of Purl Fabrics


1. Texture: Purl fabrics have a bumpy texture on the front side, caused by the horizontal bars created by the purl stitches. This texture contrasts with the smooth "V" pattern seen in knit fabrics.

2. Reversible: Many purl stitch-based patterns are reversible, meaning both sides look similar or have unique yet appealing textures on each side.

3. Stretch and Elasticity: Purl fabrics generally have more stretch and elasticity compared to plain knit fabrics, especially in the horizontal direction.

4. Thickness: These fabrics can be slightly thicker and denser, depending on the stitch pattern used.
